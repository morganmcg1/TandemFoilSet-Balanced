## Context: What Has Been Ruled Out

Before proposing new ideas, the agent has synthesized the failure modes from all 10 compound experiments and prior closed PRs:

- **SmoothL1 β sweeps**: β=0.05 is near-optimal on FiLM-Re; both β=0.02 and β=0.01 regress.
- **Depth/width scaling (mlp_ratio=4, larger hidden)**: fails at 30-min wall clock — model under-trains.
- **OneCycleLR, cosine warmup**: wall-clock budget eaten by warm-up; epochs not recovered.
- **SWA at 50% start**: too few averaging steps in 13 effective epochs to converge.
- **More Fourier bands (16)**: marginal signal, extra compute hurts.
- **Divergence-free physics loss**: fragile on FiLM-Re, high seed variance.
- **surf_weight=5**: surface overweighting hurts volume generalization enough to net-negative.
- **Geom-slice**: does not compound additively with FiLM-Re; standalone gain evaporates.
- **Multi-signal FiLM (cond_dim=5)**: val=82.46 — promising as FiLM replacement, not addition.
- **Batch size / LR scaling**: converges differently, worse at 30-min budget.

**Core binding constraint**: 30-min wall clock → ~13-14 of 50 epochs. Any mechanism that adds computation per step or adds warm-up phases pays a steep tax.

**Primary bottleneck diagnosis**: The model is not architecture-limited (Transolver is expressive) — it is *sample-complexity-limited at the 13-epoch boundary*. The FiLM-Re win came from a better inductive bias (conditioning on Re), not more capacity. The next tier of gains will come from:
1. Stronger inductive biases that reduce the effective learning problem, OR
2. Better signal quality per gradient step (loss, normalization, conditioning), OR
3. Inference-time techniques that cost zero training compute.

### Idea 1: Per-Sample Re-Scaled Normalization in Normalized Space

**Rank: 1 (highest impact/risk ratio)**
**Impact: Medium-Large | Risk: Low | Recommendation: Single arm first, then sweep**

**Hypothesis.** The current global normalization (`stats.json` y_mean / y_std) is computed over all training samples, but within a single domain the per-sample y std varies by an order of magnitude (val_single max std = 2,077 vs. mean std = 458). High-Re samples dominate the gradient signal even after Huber capping because they produce the largest absolute residuals. A per-sample normalization — dividing the loss contribution of each sample by its own y standard deviation before aggregating — would equalize gradient contributions across the Re range without any architectural change and without requiring additional training time. This is the ML analogue of "instance normalization of the loss" and is widely used in multi-scale regression tasks. It directly targets the failure mode that motivated FiLM-Re: Re-scale dependence. Unlike FiLM-Re (which conditions the model weights), this directly conditions the *learning objective* to be Re-invariant.

**Implementation.** In `train.py` compute per-sample y_std (over valid nodes) at the start of each batch. Normalize the per-node Huber loss by this per-sample std before averaging. Critically, this must use the *denormalized* y (or an equivalent scale factor computed from the normalized residuals and global y_std). The simplest form: compute `scale = y[b][mask[b]].std(dim=0).clamp(min=1.0)` for each sample `b` in the batch (in physical units or in normalized units — either works as long as consistent). Divide the per-sample mean loss by `scale` before the batch mean. This does NOT change evaluation — scoring.py is untouched. The surface-only variant computes the scale only from `is_surface` nodes, which is more directly aligned with the metric.

**Key parameters to tune**: `clamp_min` for scale (prevents over-amplifying low-Re samples); surface-only vs. full-mesh scale; whether to apply only to the surface loss term or both.

**Why it is cheap**: no new parameters, no architecture change, ~O(B) extra scalar ops per step. No wall-clock overhead. Mechanism is falsifiable: if val_re_rand improves more than val_single_in_dist, the Re-scale equalization is working.

### Idea 2: Residual Learning over Potential Flow Analytic Baseline

**Rank: 2**
**Impact: Large | Risk: Medium | Recommendation: Single arm (residual from thin-airfoil theory)**

**Hypothesis.** The Transolver currently predicts the full flow field (Ux, Uy, p) from scratch in normalized space. For aerodynamic flows at moderate-to-high Re, the dominant structure of the pressure field is well-approximated by thin-airfoil / potential-flow theory: pressure coefficient Cp ≈ -2 * (dU/dx) / U_inf, and velocity is mostly free-stream plus a circulation perturbation. This analytic baseline is deterministic given geometry and AoA — no network needed. If the model instead *learns the residual* (delta_Cp, delta_Ux, delta_Uy) between the CFD solution and the potential-flow prediction, the learning problem simplifies dramatically: the residual is smaller in magnitude (smoother, more regular), concentrated near the boundary layer and wake, and nearly zero far from the airfoil. Reducing the prediction target magnitude directly reduces the effective loss scale, which can improve gradient signal quality at no training-compute cost. DeltaPhi (ICLR 2026) demonstrated this principle for neural operators, achieving significant improvement in data-limited regimes using residual targets. The TandemFoilSet is effectively data-limited (1,499 training samples, 13 epochs).

**Implementation.** Compute a thin-airfoil approximation for each sample using the NACA geometry descriptors already in `x` (dims 14-23: AoA, camber, position, thickness for both foils). A vectorized numpy implementation of 2D thin-airfoil theory (or Weissinger panel method) can compute Cp at each surface node given (M, P, T, AoA) in milliseconds on CPU. The residual y_target = y_cfd - y_analytic. The model predicts the residual in normalized space; at inference, add back the analytic prediction.

**Key risk**: implementing a correct thin-airfoil panel method is non-trivial. A simpler fallback: use a linear regression (fit from training data on geometry/Re → y, trained once offline) as the baseline, and have the model predict the residual from this linear fit. This is a pure data-driven analytic baseline, requires no aerodynamics code, and the linear regression can be pre-computed and stored as a lookup table keyed by the 11 condition dims.

**Expected observable**: val_geom_camber_* splits should benefit most (geometry interpolation; the analytic baseline interpolates well in geometry space). val_re_rand less so (Re nonlinearity is not captured by thin-airfoil theory).

### Idea 3: Surface-Dedicated Sub-Network (Boundary-First Two-Stage Prediction)

**Rank: 3**
**Impact: Large | Risk: Medium | Recommendation: Single arm**

**Hypothesis.** The primary metric is `mae_surf_p` — surface pressure MAE. The Transolver predicts Ux, Uy, p simultaneously at all ~100K-200K nodes, of which only a small fraction (~2-5%) are surface nodes. The surface loss term is weighted equally to the volume loss in the training objective. There is no mechanism that specializes any part of the network specifically for the surface nodes that drive the metric. The boundary-GNN literature (Jena et al. arxiv March 2025) demonstrated that boundary-specialized processing dramatically reduces model size and improves accuracy for surface quantities. A two-stage approach: Stage 1 runs the existing Transolver over all nodes to produce a rough field; Stage 2 takes only surface nodes (2-5K per sample) plus the Stage 1 surface predictions as input and refines them with a lightweight MLP or shallow transformer. Stage 2 operates on ~100x fewer nodes than Stage 1, so it costs almost nothing (1-2ms per sample). The refinement network can be deeper / wider than what fits in the Transolver budget for all nodes, and it specializes entirely on the metric-relevant surface. This is architecturally orthogonal to FiLM-Re conditioning.

**Implementation.** After the existing Transolver forward pass (which now serves as a "coarse field encoder"), extract `preds[is_surface]` and `x[is_surface]` (including geometry features). Pass through a 3-layer MLP with hidden_dim=256 (or a 2-layer attention block over the ~2-5K surface tokens). Predict a *delta* correction to the surface pressure prediction. Apply the delta only to surface nodes in the final output. Loss: keep the existing combined loss for Stage 1; add a pure surface MAE loss term (weight=1.0) on the Stage 2 corrected surface predictions. Stage 2 adds ~65K params and virtually no VRAM overhead given the small surface node count.

**Key parameters**: Stage 2 hidden_dim (128 vs. 256 vs. 512), whether Stage 2 predicts delta or full output, whether Stage 2 sees geometry features only or geometry + Stage 1 residual features.

**Risk mitigation**: If the two-stage approach is complex, a simpler version is to add a Surface Refinement Head — a single extra MLP head that takes Transolver hidden states at surface nodes and predicts a surface-pressure correction. This is a 3-line change with ~16K params.

### Idea 4: Hypernetwork Re/Geometry Conditioning (Weight-Generating Conditioning)

**Rank: 4**
**Impact: Large | Risk: Medium-High | Recommendation: Single arm with careful init**

**Hypothesis.** FiLM-Re conditioning applies per-channel affine transforms (gamma, beta) to the hidden representation — this is an *additive* modulation. Over 5 Transolver layers, FiLM applies 2 × n_hidden = 256 scalar shifts per layer. A hypernetwork approach instead generates the *weights* of one or more key linear projections in the Transolver as a function of the conditioning vector (Re + NACA geometry). This is strictly more expressive than FiLM: FiLM is equivalent to a hypernetwork that generates only diagonal (scalar-broadcast) weight matrices. Hypernetwork Re conditioning was shown by Rackauckas et al. (ICLR 2026) to reduce errors by 55-72% over FiLM-equivalent baselines for parameterized PDE families. The 50x Re range (100K to 5M) is large enough that a single model with FiLM may not adequately capture the flow regime transition from laminar-like to turbulent-like; a hypernetwork that generates regime-specific projection matrices could capture this.

**Implementation.** Target the most Re-sensitive operation in Transolver: the value projection in PhysicsAttention (`self.to_v`, shape `[n_hidden, n_hidden]`). A small hypernetwork (2-layer MLP with hidden=64) takes the 1-dim log(Re) input and generates a flattened weight matrix of shape `[n_hidden * n_hidden]`, reshaped and applied as a matrix multiply. For the full n_hidden=128 case this generates 128×128=16K values per forward pass — feasible but non-trivial. A low-rank approximation (generate A [128,8] and B [8,128], then W = A@B + W_base) reduces this to 2×128×8=2K generated values. FiLM-Re stays in place for the other layers; hypernetwork targets only the attention value projection in layers 3-4 (deepest layers, most regime-specific).

**Key risk**: hypernetworks are sensitive to initialization and can destabilize training. Start with the low-rank (rank=4 or 8) variant, initialize the hypernetwork output near identity (zero-init output layer, add residual). If unstable, fall back to rank-1 (outer product).

**Why not just try larger cond_dim FiLM?** Multi-signal FiLM (cond_dim=5) gave val=82.46 — still worse than FiLM-Re alone. The issue is not the number of conditioning signals but the expressivity of the conditioning mechanism.

### Idea 5: Multiscale Mesh Pooling with Domain-Aware Graph Coarsening

**Rank: 5**
**Impact: Large | Risk: High | Recommendation: Staged — start with coarsening module only**

**Hypothesis.** The overset mesh structure (3 zones: background coarse + 2 dense foil zones) maps naturally to a 2-level hierarchy. The Transolver treats all nodes uniformly via soft slice assignment — it has no explicit mechanism to distinguish coarse-background nodes (which carry long-range flow information) from dense-zone nodes (which carry near-wall boundary-layer information). A multiscale architecture that processes these at different resolutions — global background attention + local dense-zone attention + cross-scale message passing — mirrors the MNO (ICLR 2026) architecture that achieved 5-40% error reduction on CFD tasks with up to 300K nodes. For the TandemFoilSet, the zone membership can be inferred from spatial position (Zone 0 background covers the full domain; Zones 1 and 2 are tight bounding boxes around the foils). A differentiable coarsening that pools background nodes into a compact set of "macro-tokens" and processes foil-zone nodes at full resolution would reduce the effective sequence length by 3-5x, enabling deeper or wider processing within the same compute budget.

**Implementation.** Use the `(x, z)` position features (dims 0-1) to assign each node to a zone: nodes within radius R of either foil centroid (computable from NACA geometry in dims 15-23) belong to dense zones; all others to background. Pool background nodes into N_bg "macro-tokens" via learned k-means (differentiable with straight-through, or fixed with feature averaging). Run PhysicsAttention on: [N_bg macro-tokens + N_surf surface tokens + N_sample sampled interior tokens]. Cross-scale attention from macro-tokens back to background nodes at the final layer. This is a significant architectural change (~400 lines of new code) — high risk.

**Staged approach (preferred)**: First, test whether pooling the background zone into 512 macro-tokens (vs. sampling uniformly) changes val. If it does, proceed with full multiscale. If not, close direction.

**Key risk**: zone identification from position is imperfect without explicit zone IDs (which are not in the preprocessed features). An approximation using distance from the nearest foil center (computable from dims 0-1 and 15-23) is reasonable but not exact.

### Idea 6: Stochastic Depth / LayerDrop Regularization

**Rank: 6**
**Impact: Small-Medium | Risk: Low | Recommendation: Sweep (drop_rate in {0.05, 0.10, 0.15})**

**Hypothesis.** With only 13 effective epochs, the 5-layer Transolver may be over-fitting early on the surface pressure of the training distribution while under-fitting the OOD splits (camber_rc, camber_cruise). Stochastic depth (LayerDrop) randomly skips transformer layers during training with probability p. This acts as an aggressive regularizer and also implicitly trains ensembles of sub-networks of varying depth. The wall-clock cost per epoch is *lower* with stochastic depth (some layers are skipped), so the 30-min budget can cover slightly more epochs. At inference, all layers are active. This is a well-established trick in vision transformers (Touvron et al., DeiT) with consistent OOD generalization improvements reported. The key split to watch is val_geom_camber_* (OOD geometry splits) — if those improve more than val_single_in_dist, the regularization is hitting the right target.

**Implementation.** In the Transolver forward pass, for each of the 5 PhysicsAttention blocks, sample a Bernoulli(1 - p) gate during training. If gate=0, skip the block (residual connection passes directly). At inference p=0. Implementation: wrap each `layer(x)` call with `x = x + gate * (layer(x) - x)`. Straight-through gradient. Drop probability follows a linear schedule by depth (deeper layers are more likely to be dropped, following Huang et al. 2016): layer_i drop rate = p * (i / n_layers).

**Key hyperparameter**: drop_rate. Sweep {0.05, 0.10, 0.15}. Start with 0.10 as it is most commonly reported as optimal for 5-6 layer networks.

### Idea 7: Checkpoint Weight Averaging over Training Run (Zero Training Cost)

**Rank: 7**
**Impact: Small-Medium | Risk: Very Low | Recommendation: Single arm, inference-only change**

**Hypothesis.** The current checkpoint selection saves only the single best-val-epoch model. Weight averaging over the last K checkpoints (EWA or uniform) is a known technique for improving generalization without any additional training. In the 13-epoch regime, epoch variance is high — the best checkpoint fluctuates by ±2 epochs between runs. Averaging weights from epochs 10-14 (the typical best-checkpoint window) produces a model that sits at the center of the loss basin rather than at a potentially lucky local minimum. This costs zero training compute and zero VRAM. It only requires saving K additional checkpoint files during training (or maintaining a running EWA of weights). Expected improvement: 1-3% reduction in val_avg based on standard SWA ablations for short-training-budget regimes. The difference from the failed SWA (edward #3669, val=102.24) is critical: that experiment started SWA too early (50% start = epoch 7) and used the SWA scheduler (cyclical LR), both of which disrupted the well-tuned FiLM-Re training dynamics. This proposal uses uniform weight averaging of checkpoints *already saved by the training harness* — no LR schedule change, no SWA scheduler, purely a post-hoc averaging of saved weights.

**Implementation.** During training, save a checkpoint every epoch for the last K=5 epochs. After training completes, load all K checkpoints, average their state_dicts parameter-by-parameter (simple sum / K for each tensor), evaluate the averaged model on val splits. Report the averaged model's val metric vs. the single best-epoch model. If the averaged model is better, use it as the final eval model. No change to the training loop itself — purely a post-processing step.

**Why this differs from failed SWA**: SWA PR used a cyclic LR schedule starting at epoch 7, which destabilized training dynamics and hurt val at epoch 13. This proposal uses *checkpoint post-averaging only* — no LR schedule change, averaging only in the final 5 epochs when the model is well-converged.

### Idea 8: Multi-Task Learning with Volume Field Consistency Loss

**Rank: 8**
**Impact: Medium | Risk: Low-Medium | Recommendation: Single arm**

**Hypothesis.** The model currently minimizes a surface-weighted SmoothL1 loss that includes both volume and surface terms. However, the *physical relationship* between surface pressure and the velocity field is encoded by Bernoulli's equation (in potential flow: p + 0.5*rho*|U|^2 = const along streamlines) and by the Navier-Stokes momentum equation. These physical constraints mean that a correct surface pressure prediction is *not independent* of correct velocity predictions. Adding an auxiliary consistency loss — `| p_pred + 0.5*(Ux_pred^2 + Uy_pred^2) - C |^2` over surface nodes, where C is the free-stream Bernoulli constant derived from the far-field boundary condition — penalizes predictions that violate energy conservation. This soft physics constraint regularizes the pressure prediction without requiring external solvers and provides additional gradient signal about p's relationship to (Ux, Uy). The coupling between channels (which the model currently predicts independently) is the signal the constraint exploits.

**Implementation.** After computing pred_phys (denormalized predictions), compute `bernoulli_residual = pred_phys[:, :, 2] + 0.5 * (pred_phys[:, :, 0]**2 + pred_phys[:, :, 1]**2)` at surface nodes (use `is_surface` mask). The free-stream value C = 0.5 * U_inf^2, where U_inf can be approximated from the far-field velocity encoded in x (or set to a fixed value per domain from meta.json). Add `bernoulli_loss = bernoulli_residual.var(dim=-1)[mask].mean()` as an auxiliary term — minimizing the variance of the Bernoulli sum over surface nodes enforces streamline consistency. Weight: bernoulli_weight=0.01 to 0.1 (sweep). Note: this is an *approximation* — Bernoulli holds for inviscid flow; viscous corrections are significant near the boundary layer. But as a soft regularizer it provides useful signal.

**Key risk**: the Bernoulli approximation breaks down in the boundary layer and wake, which are exactly the regions with the highest surface pressure gradients. The loss may penalize physically correct predictions. Mitigation: apply only to the upstream (pressure-side) surface nodes, or use a loose weight (0.001) and monitor whether the auxiliary loss actually decreases.

## Research State Update

**Current best explanation for plateau**: The FiLM-Re gain came from correcting Re-scale sensitivity of the learning objective (implicit via weight modulation). All compound experiments added complexity without addressing this fundamental issue more directly. The next tier of gains likely comes from either (a) directly normalizing the loss for Re-scale (Idea 1), or (b) reducing the target magnitude via residual learning (Idea 2), or (c) specializing network capacity on the surface nodes that determine the primary metric (Idea 3).

**Open uncertainties**:
1. Is the 13-epoch regime fundamentally sample-complexity-limited (not fixable by architecture), or is there an architecture that learns faster per epoch?
2. Does the surface vs. volume trade-off in the loss explain why surf_weight=5 hurt? (Too aggressive; moderate surface emphasis may still help.)
3. Are the OOD camber splits (val_geom_camber_rc, val_geom_camber_cruise) correlated with each other or orthogonal — meaning can we improve both simultaneously, or are they in tension?

**Stop condition**: If Ideas 1, 3, and 2 all fail to beat the FiLM-Re baseline, escalate to Idea 5 (multiscale architecture) or consider a fundamentally different model family (e.g., SpiderSolver-style boundary-first tokenization, GFocal neural operator).
